    • Salvatore Barone is Tenured Assistant Professor (RTT L. 240/2010, scientific area IINF-05/A - Information Processing Systems) at the Department of Computer Science and Technologies, Pegaso University, Naples, Italy. He attained his PhD in Information Technology and Electrical Engineering from University of Naples Federico II, Naples, Italy, in 2022.
    His research activities include the of design of high-performance computing systems, embedded systems, and safety-critical systems. He participates to research activities of the Center for Artificial Intelligence and Extended Reality at Pegaso University.

    He serves (or served) as an Guest Editor for Special Issue Future Generation Computer Systems, Elsevier, Electronics, MDPI
    He served as a General Chair for the 10th Workshop on Approximate Computing, the 9th Workshop on Approximate Computing.
    He has been part of the tecnical program committee of several internationa conferences, including Design, Automation and Test in Europe (DATE-2026), 31th Asia and South Pacific Design Automation Conference (ASP-DAC 2026), 16th IEEE Latin American Symposium on Circuits and Systems (LASCAS2025), Design, Automation and Test in Europe (DATE-2025), 30th Asia and South Pacific Design Automation Conference (ASP-DAC 2025).

    Teaching

    Laboratorio prompt engineering e LLM (ING-INF/05)

    The objective of this course is to provide the theory, techniques, tips, and tricks necessary to master prompt engineering and build applications based on Large Language Models.
    Learning objectives: fundamental understanding of LLMs, their inner workings, and their functionality as text completion engines; developing applications based on Large Language Models; fundamental techniques for prompt engineering; advanced techniques for assembling LLM-based loops, pipelines, and inference workflows to create conversational agents and LLM-driven workflows; techniques and metrics for evaluating LLMs.

    Basi di dati (ING-INF/05)

    The course aims to analyze the main models and languages for defining and querying databases, with particular attention to the relational model. The course explores database design methodologies in the conceptual, logical, and physical phases, and introduces recent developments in models, languages, and systems in the context of computer applications.
    Learning objectives: analyze the main database models; explore database query languages; design databases in the conceptual, logical, and physical phases.
